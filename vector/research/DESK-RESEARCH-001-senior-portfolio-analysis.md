# DESK-RESEARCH-001 — Senior Designer Portfolio Analysis

**Date:** 2026-03-25
**Status:** Complete
**Scope:** How top UX/Product designers at FAANG and Fortune 100 companies structure their portfolios. What separates senior from junior. Applied findings to Rob's portfolio copy and structure.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. The Minto Pyramid structure outperforms chronological process documentation
Hiring managers read outcomes first, process second. The strongest portfolios (Simon Pan, Elise Fu) put quantified results at the top of both the homepage card and the case study. Process documentation follows as supporting evidence.

### 2. Problem reframing is the senior signal, not the final UI
Simon Pan's Uber case study pivots from "improve pickup" to "poorly formed rendezvous plans cause downstream problems." The reframing moment is the narrative climax. Rob's APM tab-vs-modular-structure story has this same quality and should be more prominent.

### 3. Forced metrics undercut credibility
"7 of 10 heuristics violated" or "5 flows in 1 month" sound like reaching for numbers. Strong portfolios use real metrics where they exist (SUS scores, view counts, revenue impact) and specific descriptive outcomes where they don't. Never vague adjectives ("improved usability").

### 4. Case study structure should vary by project type
Enterprise SaaS (Honeywell) should emphasize stakeholder navigation, research rigor, and constraints. Zero-to-one products (Keytrn, Aysa) should emphasize product thinking, speed, and full-stack ownership. Same template for every project is a junior signal.

### 5. The portfolio site itself is a design artifact
For founder audiences (PER-002), the fact that Rob built the site in Astro/React/Framer Motion is a differentiator. Multiple sources confirmed: founders evaluate the portfolio's craft as evidence of the designer's technical capability.

### 6. Naming collaborators signals seniority
"Designs in collaboration with [role]" shows cross-functional work and generosity. Saying "we" without attribution makes it impossible to assess individual contribution. Saying "I" for everything looks like overclaiming.
